# Chatbot-Sentiment-Analysis
This project synthesizes current evidence on the clinical effectiveness of mental health chatbots its  design and deployment.

Background: Despite growing recognition of mental health as a global public health priority, access to timely and affordable care remains limited by structural factors. Clinician shortages and rising demand have accelerated the adoption of AI-enabled mental health chatbots, which deliver psychoeducation and therapy-inspired interventions through rule-based systems and increasingly large language models (LLMs).
Objective: This review synthesizes current evidence on the clinical effectiveness of mental health chatbots and critically examines the ethical risks associated with their design and deployment.

Methods: A structured search of PubMed, PsycINFO, and Scopus (2018–2026) identified 47 peer-reviewed studies, including meta-analyses, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), systematic reviews, and ethical analyses.

Results: Meta-analytic evidence suggests small-to-moderate short-term improvements in depression, anxiety, and psychological distress (Cohen's d ≈ 0.3–0.6), particularly among young people with mild-to-moderate symptoms. However, these findings must be interpreted cautiously due to methodological limitations, including short intervention durations, small sample sizes, and reliance on self-reported outcomes. Evidence directly comparing LLM-based chatbots with active psychotherapeutic controls remains limited. Ethical concerns consistently emerge across four domains: (a) inadequate crisis response—for example, one audit found chatbots failed to escalate 78% of explicit suicidal disclosures (De Freitas et al., 2023); (b) opaque data practices and risks to privacy and confidentiality; (c) algorithmic bias that may disadvantage culturally diverse or low-literacy populations; and (d) anthropomorphisation leading to therapeutic misconception, where users overestimate chatbot expertise. At the system level, concerns arise regarding the substitution of automated tools for human care in under-resourced settings.

Conclusion: Mental health chatbots can function as valuable adjuncts within stepped or hybrid care models, expanding access and offering low-barrier support. However, current evidence does not support their use as stand-alone replacements for professional treatment, particularly in high-risk contexts. Responsible implementation requires rigorous clinical validation, robust crisis protocols, transparent data governance, bias mitigation strategies, and clear communication of therapeutic limitations.
Keywords: mental health chatbots; AI; effectiveness; ethics; safety; privacy; stepped care
